Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
Spontaneous resolution by adulthood in majority (persistent adult acne in 12-22% of women, 3% of men); moderate-severe acne rarely self-resolves; scarring occurs in up to 95% without adequate treatment
Best-intervention remission
Oral isotretinoin: 85-90% achieve sustained remission (acne-free at 5 years) after one course; relapse requiring retreat in 15-20%; topical adapalene + benzoyl peroxide: 65-70% reduction in inflammatory lesions at 12 weeks

Gap size
Large — isotretinoin is highly effective but dramatically underused due to iPLEDGE programme barriers and pregnancy concerns; patients spend years on antibiotics instead
Primary barrier
access/framing
Barrier detail: The iPLEDGE programme creates substantial barriers to isotretinoin access (monthly blood tests, pregnancy tests, pharmacy restrictions); this leads to years of antibiotic courses creating antibiotic resistance and allowing scarring to progress; acne is not considered a 'serious' disease despite causing significant psychological morbidity and permanent scarring

Last SoC change: 2022 — FDA approved clascoterone (Winlevi) — first androgen receptor inhibitor for acne; 2021 Accutane biosimilar access improved; 2019 AAD guidelines updated; isotretinoin approved 1982; tetracycline antibiotics for acne since 1950s
