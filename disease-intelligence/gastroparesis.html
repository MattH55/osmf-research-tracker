Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
Idiopathic gastroparesis: spontaneous resolution in ~30-40% at 5 years (NIDDK natural history study); diabetic gastroparesis: rarely remits, correlated with glycaemic control; postsurgical: highly variable
Best-intervention remission
Dietary modification + glycaemic optimisation: 50-60% improvement in symptoms; metoclopramide: 30-40% short-term symptom improvement (limited by side effects); gastric electrical stimulation (Enterra Therapy): 50-60% significant symptom improvement at 3-5 years in refractory patients; pyloric procedures (G-POEM): 75-80% short-term improvement

Gap size
Large — gastric electrical stimulation and pyloromyotomy are underused; metoclopramide misuse is the dominant pharmacotherapy problem
Primary barrier
practice/access
Barrier detail: Metoclopramide (the only FDA-approved drug for gastroparesis since 1979) carries a black box warning for tardive dyskinesia with >12 weeks use; yet many patients are maintained long-term; gastric electrical stimulation (GES) is underutilised and restricted to few centres; domperidone (off-label, superior safety) inaccessible in US except via FDA expanded access

Last SoC change: 2023 — G-POEM (gastric per-oral endoscopic pyloromyotomy) increasingly adopted; trazpiroben (Phase 3 GASS trial ongoing) and relamorelin (Phase 3 failed primary endpoint 2018) attempted; metoclopramide as sole approved drug since 1979 — one of the longest-unchanged pharmacotherapy landscapes in GI medicine
