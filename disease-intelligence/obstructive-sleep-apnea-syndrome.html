Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
Spontaneous resolution without intervention rare; weight loss of 10-15% reduces AHI by 25-50% in obese patients; mild OSA may resolve with weight loss; moderate-severe OSA essentially irreversible without intervention
Best-intervention remission
CPAP: eliminates apnoeic events (AHI <5) in 95%+ when used; improves BP 2-3 mmHg, Epworth sleepiness, QoL; mandibular advancement device (MAD): reduces AHI 50-60% in mild-moderate, less effective in severe; bariatric surgery: AHI reduction 80-85% (OSA resolution in 40-50%); hypoglossal nerve stimulation (Inspire): AHI reduction 68% at 12 months

Gap size
Very large — CPAP adherence is the dominant problem; 30-50% of CPAP-prescribed patients are non-adherent; alternative approaches (MAD, positional therapy, weight loss) underused in non-adherent patients
Primary barrier
practice/adherence
Barrier detail: CPAP adherence is the single largest challenge in sleep medicine; 50% of patients discontinue CPAP within 1-3 years; inadequate follow-up support and lack of alternatives to CPAP for non-adherent patients; hypoglossal nerve stimulation (Inspire) is highly effective in CPAP-intolerant patients but vastly underutilised

Last SoC change: 2024 — tirzepatide showing 60-70% AHI reduction in obese OSA patients (SURMOUNT-OSA trial); 2023 Inspire (HNS) expanded indications; 2014 Inspire FDA approved; CPAP as standard since 1981; essentially unchanged paradigm for 40 years in most clinical settings
