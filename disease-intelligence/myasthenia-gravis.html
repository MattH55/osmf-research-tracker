Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
Spontaneous complete stable remission (CSR) in 10-20% of patients over 10 years; generalised MG rarely resolves without treatment; ocular MG may remain limited in 50-60%
Best-intervention remission
Thymectomy in non-thymomatous MG at age <65: 50% more likely to achieve CSR at 3 years vs medical therapy (MGTX trial); eculizumab (ravulizumab): 40% reduction in exacerbations in AChR+ MG; efgartigimod alfa (Vyvgart): 67.7% MG-ADL responders (ADAPT trial); zilucoplan: 53% MG-ADL response (RAISE trial)

Gap size
Large — new complement inhibitors and FcRn antagonists represent genuine advances but cost and access are barriers; thymectomy underused in appropriate candidates
Primary barrier
access
Barrier detail: Thymectomy (MGTX trial proven benefit) is delayed or not offered in many patients with non-thymomatous MG; eculizumab ($700K/year) dramatically limits access; efgartigimod and zilucoplan more accessible but still $200K+/year; corticosteroids cause significant morbidity in MG patients managed long-term

Last SoC change: 2023 — rozanolixizumab (Rystiggo, FcRn inhibitor) approved; zilucoplan (Zilbrysq, complement C5 inhibitor) 2023; efgartigimod alfa (Vyvgart) 2021; eculizumab 2017; thymectomy MGTX trial 2016; pyridostigmine/corticosteroids standard since 1950s-1960s

Right to Try Relevance Critical

Eligibility: This disease meets the federal and state right-to-try legal standard (21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-0a and Montana SB 535): it lacks disease-modifying therapy with indication-specific FDA approval. Patients with Myasthenia Gravis may be eligible to access investigational therapies outside of clinical trials under right-to-try frameworks.
