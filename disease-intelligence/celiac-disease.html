Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
No spontaneous remission — autoimmune injury continues as long as gluten is consumed; villous atrophy persists in 30-40% at 5 years even on gluten-free diet (GFD) due to cross-contamination
Best-intervention remission
Strict gluten-free diet: histological remission (Marsh 0-1) in 60-70% at 2 years; mucosal healing complete in only ~40% at 5 years; non-responsive celiac in 7-30% (defined as persistent symptoms/histology on strict GFD)
Drug-free remission
—

Chronicity

Gap size
Moderate — GFD is effective but imperfect; adherence is the primary barrier; and no pharmacotherapy exists for GFD non-responders
Primary barrier
framing/biological
Barrier detail: The GFD is the only treatment and no pharmacotherapy is approved, leaving 7-30% of non-responsive/refractory patients without additional options; even 'strict' adherence fails to achieve mucosal healing in a large minority due to food labelling gaps and cross-contamination; quality of life impact of GFD is substantial; refractory celiac disease Type II (RCDII) has very poor prognosis

Last SoC change: 2023 — FALCATA Phase 3 (larazotide, tight junction modulator) — failed primary endpoint; 2023 ATLAS Phase 2 (latiglutenase, gluten-degrading enzyme) — some histological benefit; GFD has been the only treatment since celiac disease was first described in 1950s; no pharmacotherapy approved to date
