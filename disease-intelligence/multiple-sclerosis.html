Remission & chronicity

Spontaneous remission
Untreated relapsing MS: ~1 relapse/year; disability accumulates over time in ~85% who convert to secondary progressive MS; spontaneous remission from MS is not possible
Best-intervention remission
High-efficacy DMTs (natalizumab, alemtuzumab, ocrelizumab, cladribine) achieve 0 clinical relapses in 50-70% at 2 years and >95% MRI lesion suppression; early high-efficacy treatment shows superior outcomes vs platform therapy escalation

Gap size
Large — massive gap between platform DMT (interferons, glatiramer) and high-efficacy DMT outcomes, but practice still favors escalation model
Primary barrier
practice
Barrier detail: Treat-to-target with early high-efficacy therapy is superior to the traditional safety-first escalation model (TREAT-MS, DELIVER-MS trials) but most MS neurologists still start with platform therapies; hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) achieves 70-80% no-evidence-of-disease-activity (NEDA) at 5 years but is rarely offered

Last SoC change: 2023 — FDA approved tolebrutinib and fenebrutinib (BTK inhibitors) for primary progressive MS; ocrelizumab approved for PPMS 2017 (first ever treatment); platform therapies (interferons, glatiramer) unchanged since 1993-1996; paradigm shift to high-efficacy first gaining guideline endorsement only now
